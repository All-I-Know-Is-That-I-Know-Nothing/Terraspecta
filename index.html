About Contribute Atlas

One view, photographed again and again, becomes a record of colour.

The Colour Atlas is a slow, collective survey of the colours of places. Contributors photograph the same view, extract its palette, and add it to a shared map — without ever sharing the photographs themselves.

The Colour Atlas began as a weekly ritual: twenty photographs of the same stretch of the Thames, taken from the same place, run through a small pipeline that distils them into colour. Over months, the palettes became a diary of weather, season and light — a way of seeing a familiar view change.

Now the pipeline is open. Anyone, anywhere, can photograph their own view — a window, a street corner, a horizon — and contribute its colours to a growing atlas of places, told entirely in palette.

Everything is processed in your browser. The Atlas keeps only what the colours can tell.

How it works

Four steps

  1. i.

    Photograph a view

    Around twenty photographs of the same view, taken on one day. Any camera, any weather.

  2. ii.

    Generate the palettes

    Your browser reads every pixel and produces two studies: a sky/earth palette and a percentages palette.

  3. iii.

    Place it, loosely

    Name the place and country yourself, and drop an approximate pin. Coordinates are snapped to a coarse grid before anything is sent.

  4. iv.

    Join the Atlas

    Your palette appears as a strip of colour on the public map — alongside, if you like, a thought from the view.

What leaves your device

Sent to the Atlas

  • The extracted palette data (colours & percentages)
  • The sky / earth ratio
  • The place name and country you typed
  • The snapped grid cell of your pin
  • Your optional thought

Never sent

  • Your photographs
  • Embedded camera or GPS metadata
  • Your exact coordinates
  • Your name or email
